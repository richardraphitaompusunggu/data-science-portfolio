HR Analytics · Synthetic 2M-row Benchmark · July 2026

A production-scale HR workflow, built honestly on a rule-generated dataset

Path A (descriptive) + Path B (predictive benchmark) · 2,000,000 employee records · 7 European markets

Executive Summary

Situation. The team needs proof that an HR analytics stack — composition, pay, attrition, hiring, and a churn model — runs cleanly at 2,000,000-row scale.

Complication. The dataset is rule-generated: salaries were median-filled by Level×Department, ages were formula-derived, ratings were stratified-sampled, and Status was assigned independently. There is no snapshot date and no gender field. Naïve analysis will surface these construction artifacts as if they were insight.

The dataset is a technique benchmark, not a source of HR insight: the pipeline reproduces the documented cleaning, reports honest descriptive metrics at scale, and demonstrates which predictive tasks are legitimate (attrition) versus artifacts (salary, performance) — portfolio-ready without overclaiming.
  1. Reproducible cleaning. All three documented raw defect classes — 3,333 null ratings, 3,333 non-positive salaries, 164,794 age–experience violations — resolve to zero with the author's stated techniques; the clean file passes every business rule.
  2. Honest description at scale. Workforce is bottom-heavy (76% Junior/Mid), pay is a clean ~4.5× Director-over-Junior ladder in every department, attrition is flat across departments but triples at the Junior level (~17% vs ~5% senior), and hiring compounds ~18%/yr (2009–2025).
  3. Predictive honesty. An attrition classifier extracts modest, seniority-driven signal (ROC-AUC ≈ 0.66, beating the 0.50 baseline); a salary model's near-perfect R² is exposed as a median-fill artifact; and a performance model correctly finds no signal.

Adopt this as a benchmarking / technique asset. Do not use it for pay-equity or attrition-driver decisions until Data Engineering adds a real snapshot date and a gender field to the schema.

Part I · Composition & Pay

Key Line 1 — Sales and IT hold half the workforce; the pyramid is bottom-heavy

So What: programs should be sized to the 76% Junior/Mid front line, not the 5% Director tier — and metrics should use medians, since a thin high-paid tail distorts means.

Key Line 1 (cont.) — Pay is a clean 4-rung ladder, ~4.5× top-to-bottom

So What: salary is monotonic Junior→Director in all five departments — internally consistent, but a cleaning artifact of the Level×Dept median-fill, not evidence of a market-calibrated pay policy.
Part II · Attrition & Hiring

Key Line 2 — Attrition is flat across departments but triples at the Junior level

So What: the real axis of attrition is seniority/tenure — Junior staff churn at 16.6% versus ~4–6% at senior levels, while every department sits within ±0.3pp of the 10.3% mean. Retention effort belongs on the early-career population.

Key Line 2 (cont.) — Hiring compounds ~18%/yr; 2008 and 2026 are edge artifacts

So What: 2009–2025 is a usable growth/seasonality series; the 2008 backfill spike and truncated 2026 must be excluded before any trend claim.

Appendix · Methodology & Limitations

Pipeline

Stage 1 ingestion (schema + reconciliation) → Stage 2 cleaning (raw-defect reproduction, rule validation, 8 derived features) → Stage 3 EDA (5 descriptive cuts) → Stage 5a/Path B (effect sizes + 3 model benchmarks) → Stage 7 report. All notebooks are reproducible (random_state=42); models use a 200k analytic sample. Charts follow the project's McKinsey design system.

Data reality & limitations

Synthetic, rule-generated data (2,000,000 rows, 16 columns, 0 nulls). Hard limits: (1) Year == Hire_Year for every row — no snapshot date, so current tenure and time-to-attrition are uncomputable; (2) no Gender field — pay-equity/DEI is out of scope; (3) salary is a near-deterministic function of Level×Department; (4) Status is independently assigned; (5) the stated age-gap floor is 22 but the file only achieves 21. Currency is unspecified (European cities suggest EUR).
