adidas · global merchandising diagnostic · July 2026

adidas holds one price ladder across markets — the divergence that matters is in stock and range, not price or promotion

Situation. adidas runs a single global brand through 10 market storefronts, each with its own currency, language, and localized catalogue — some divergence in price and range is expected.

Complication. It was unclear how much the four commercial levers — price, assortment, stock, discount — actually diverge across markets, because the data is size-level, multi-currency, and mixed-language.

Resolution. Once normalized to USD and one taxonomy, adidas is broadly consistent: the real, actionable divergence is concentrated in availability and assortment, while pricing is a narrow tail story and discounting is effectively absent.

Availability and assortment — not price or discount — are where adidas's markets genuinely diverge; that is where central merchandising attention pays off.
  1. Availability is the sharpest divergence. Size coverage varies most across markets (large effect, ε²=0.30); Tennis runs thinnest (~50%), and the pattern is predictable at ROC-AUC 0.76.
  2. Assortment blends a global core with local tilts (Cramér's V=0.35, moderate): Lifestyle + Running dominate everywhere, over specific market-led categories.
  3. Price is broadly harmonized — the same product varies only ~7% across markets; only a tail diverges (MX dearest, BR cheapest).
  4. Discounting is a non-lever — just 0.2% of SKUs carry a real markdown; six of ten markets show none.
Recommendation. Merchandising should (1) stand up an availability watchlist from the classifier to pre-empt stock-outs in thin category×market cells; (2) plan the global core range centrally while preserving the flagged local tilts; and (3) run a price review on the tail of high-dispersion SKUs (and the anomaly shortlist) — not a market-wide reprice. Discount policy needs no action.

Part I · Diagnostic — the four levers

1. Availability diverges most — and is structurally predictable

Stock coverage (available sizes ÷ total sizes) differs across markets with a large effect (Kruskal-Wallis ε²=0.30). Tennis shows the thinnest median coverage (~50%) — a proxy for demand pressure or deliberate scarcity, not proof of either.

Because the pattern is systematic, a leakage-clean classifier predicts availability from catalog attributes alone at ROC-AUC 0.76 (PR-AUC 0.84, F1 0.78) — beating a logistic baseline and the majority class. Market and size-range breadth carry the signal.

So What. Availability is both the biggest cross-market gap and the most operationally tractable: score the live catalogue to flag at-risk SKUs before they sell through. Source: 44,888 size-level rows, 2026-02-28 & 03-09. Predictive, not causal.

2. A global core, tilted locally by market

Category mix is moderately dependent on market (Cramér's V=0.35; χ² p<0.001): a Lifestyle + Running core appears everywhere, over sharp local tilts (e.g. football-heavy LATAM/EU, and China's large uncategorized band).

So What. The shared core can be planned centrally; the flagged tilts are the deliberately-local ranges. The split tells merchandising precisely what to harmonize vs. localize. Source: canonical category mapping (53 raw → 17 labels). China's 'No Category' = 57% missing, kept in-scope.

3. Price is broadly harmonized — divergence lives in the tail

For the 493 products sold in ≥2 markets, USD price varies only ~7% at the median — adidas runs essentially one global ladder. A long tail is the exception: MX prices the same goods dearest, BR cheapest.

A price model (R²=0.45, MAE $26) turns this into a screen: its residual tails shortlist SKUs priced far from the market norm for a human pricing review.

So What. There is no market-wide repricing case; target the tail. Use the anomaly screen as a shortlist, not an automated re-pricer. Source: price_usd via reference FX (see caveat). CoV ranking is FX-robust; absolute USD is not.

4. Discounting is effectively absent

The anticipated "who discounts most" question dissolves: only 0.2% of SKUs carry a real markdown, and six of ten markets show none at all. Where markdowns appear (KR, DE, GB, MX) they are deep (~35%) but touch a vanishing slice of the range.

So What. Discount is not a strategic lever in this snapshot — no action needed. A statistically significant market difference here is practically negligible (ε²=0.005). Source: recomputed discount (source field ignored; 19,012 inconsistent rows corrected).
Part II · What the data cannot answer

This is a catalogue panel — pricing, assortment, and stock at two dates — with no sales, revenue, cost, or demand data. It therefore cannot support: sales/volume analysis, revenue or margin, true demand elasticity, causal claims ("cut price to lift stock"), or a real time-series trend (two dates, nine days apart). Rating prediction was also de-scoped: price and rating are essentially unrelated (ρ≈−0.04) and only 39% of rows are rated. Every finding above is associational.

Appendix · Methodology & technical detail
FX caveat. USD conversion uses early-2026 reference rates pinned to 2026-03-09 (data/external/fx_rates.csv). Cross-market relative comparisons (CoV, price index) are robust to small rate errors; absolute USD levels are not until authoritative rates (ECB/OANDA) are pinned — this especially colours the BR read.
