Fix mobile and lean into cross-sell — ignore the landing-page mirage

An analysis of 472,871 website sessions and 32,313 orders, Mar 2012 – Mar 2015. Prepared for the Head of Growth.

Situation A young online toy retailer grew from one plush product and one paid-search channel to a four-product catalogue across devices and channels over three years; conversion improved from ~3% to ~8%.

Complication Growth was bought with traffic, but it is unclear which of channel mix, site experience, or product strategy actually drives profitable growth — and an apparent landing-page "winner" is tempting to roll out.

Resolution The durable levers are on-site and in the product mix, not in channel reshuffling; and the landing-page signal does not survive scrutiny.

The two growth levers worth funding are the mobile conversion gap and cross-sell of higher-margin newer products; the landing-page differences are a confounding artifact, and channel mix and refunds are secondary.
  1. Mobile is the single biggest conversion lever. Desktop converts at 8.5% versus mobile's 3.1% (2.8×), yet mobile is 31% of all traffic. This gap is large, statistically unambiguous, and needs no causal caveat to act on.
  2. Cross-sell of higher-margin newer products grows profit. Multi-item orders went from ~0% to 34% of orders after 2013, lifting average order value from $50 to $64. The flagship earns a 61% margin while newer products earn 63%+ — so steering cross-sell toward them compounds the gain.
  3. The eye-catching landing-page differences are a confounding artifact — do not act on them. Landing pages were run in different eras and targeted at different device segments; once matched like-for-like, no variant reliably beats /home. Optimising landing pages requires a proper randomised A/B test, not this observational data.
Recommendation

1. Product & UX (this quarter): commission a mobile-conversion diagnosis and redesign — closing even a third of the 2.75× gap is the largest single conversion prize.
2. Merchandising (next quarter): extend the proven cross-sell mechanic, defaulting recommendations to the higher-margin newer products.
3. Growth & Analytics (before any lander change): run a randomised landing-page A/B test within a fixed device segment; treat socialbook as a low-converting source pending spend data. Do not retire or promote any lander on the current evidence.

Part I · Conversion

Mobile converts at a third of the desktop rate — the biggest on-site prize

Desktop sessions convert at 8.5% and mobile at just 3.1% (relative risk 2.75×, non-overlapping 95% confidence intervals, p < 0.001). Mobile is 31% of traffic, so this is not a niche.

So What. Mobile is nearly a third of traffic but converts at ~1/3 the desktop rate. Closing part of this gap is the single largest conversion opportunity in the funnel.
Two-proportion z-test; Wilson 95% CIs. Source: session_summary (n=472,871).

The funnel itself corroborates a UX story: the weakest step is product→cart (only 36% of product viewers add to cart), and conversion has trended steadily upward as the site matured.

So What. Once users reach the cart, checkout completes well (62–81% per step); the leaks are upstream — landing→products and products→cart — consistent with an experience problem, which skews to mobile.
Unique sessions reaching each stage. Source: website_pageviews.
Part II · Monetization

Cross-sell of higher-margin newer products is a proven, under-exploited lever

Average order value rose from $50 to $64 as multi-item orders grew from essentially zero to 34% of orders after the second product launched in 2013.

So What. The cross-sell mechanic demonstrably works. Because newer products carry ~7–8 points more margin than the flagship, steering add-ons toward them turns a revenue lever into a margin lever.
Monthly. Source: orders. Structural note: cross-sell only possible once a 2nd product existed.
So What. Revenue is concentrated in the flagship, which is also the lowest-margin product — a portfolio-concentration risk that growing the higher-margin newer SKUs directly mitigates.
Gross margin = price − COGS. Source: order_items × products.
Part III · What the data does NOT support

The landing-page “winner” is a confounding artifact

EDA showed landing pages spanning 4.5%–10.2% conversion — a tempting lever. But the pages ran in different eras (while the site-wide rate climbed from 3% to 8%) and were targeted at different device segments (some ~100% desktop, others ~100% mobile). Matching like-for-like collapses the effect.

So What. Controlling for time alone (grey) leaves a big apparent spread; matching fully on device, visitor type, source and time (blue) collapses every lander to within ~1pp of /home, none surviving Bonferroni correction. The naive read would have wrongly retired /lander-3 and over-invested in /lander-5.
Within-window vs segment-matched two-proportion tests, Bonferroni-corrected. Source: session_summary.

This is the report's discipline check: a large, significant-looking difference that evaporates under confounding control. The right response is a prospective randomised A/B test, not a rollout.

Appendix

Methodology, data & limitations

Data & lineage. Six related tables (sessions, pageviews, orders, order_items, refunds, products), Mar 2012 – Mar 2015. Pipeline: immutable ingestion → schema & business-rule validation (13/13 passed, zero violations) → typed/cleaned Parquet with a derived per-session summary (sessionised from 1.19M pageviews) → EDA → statistical testing. Overall margin 63%; blended refund rate 4.4% of revenue.

Methods. Two-proportion z-tests and chi-square independence for conversion; effect sizes (Cohen’s h, Cramér’s V, relative risk) reported alongside every p-value; Wilson confidence intervals; Bonferroni & Benjamini–Hochberg correction on the landing-page family; a within-window + segment-matched design to control the landing-page time/device confound.

Secondary findings. Traffic source is statistically but not practically related to conversion (Cramér’s V = 0.02); only socialbook (~3.2%) is a materially weak converter. Refunds peaked near 9% on the flagship’s early-2012 batches and have since settled around 3% — a resolved issue to monitor, not a priority.

Limitations. No marketing spend → conversion quality is rankable but true CAC/ROAS is not; no geography or customer demographics; A/B tests are unlabelled (inferred from URL × time); no refund-reason codes; timestamps are timezone-naive. These bound the recommendations to on-site and product levers rather than budget-allocation claims.
