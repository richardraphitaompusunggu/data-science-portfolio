Fraud Operations · Decision Memo

Fraud is detectable at 1-in-600 odds — the constraint is review capacity, not model quality

Tier A analysis · 284,807 card transactions over 48 hours · prepared 22 July 2026

Situation A card issuer processed 284,807 transactions in 48 hours. 492 were fraudulent — 0.17%, or roughly 1 in 578. A review team can inspect only a small fraction of that volume by hand.

Complication The transaction features are PCA-anonymized, so it is not obvious that fraud is learnable at all — and the metrics normally used to judge that are actively misleading here. A model that flags nothing scores 99.83% accuracy. Even ROC-AUC, the usual fallback, reads 0.98 for a model whose precision at operating capacity is 20%. Nobody can tell from a standard report whether a scorer is worth the review labour.

Resolution It is worth it — with an important correction to how success is measured.

Fraud in this portfolio is highly detectable despite fully anonymized features — a class-weighted gradient-boosted ranker converts a 1-in-599 needle-in-a-haystack into a 400-alert daily queue that catches 86% of fraud cases and 81% of fraud value while touching 0.7% of transaction volume — so the binding constraint is not model quality but review capacity, and the model should be deployed as a queue ranker, never as an autonomous decline engine.

RecommendationThe fraud-operations lead should deploy the model as a review-queue ranker at a score threshold of 0.0009 — the top 400 of every 56,746 transactions, equivalent to 1,000 reviews/day — and not as an auto-decline. Three actions carry dates: (1) ship the zero-amount business rule immediately — it needs no model and flags 8.3x-elevated risk in 0.64% of volume; (2) measure the true cost per manual review within 30 days, since it moves the optimal threshold by an order of magnitude and is the single highest-value missing input; (3) stand up PSI drift monitoring on the top six components plus amount before go-live, and run 60 days in shadow mode to calibrate the alert thresholds this 48-hour sample cannot.
Part I · Is fraud detectable?

Accuracy certifies a model that catches nothing — which is why this analysis is measured on precision-recall

At a 0.17% base rate, the default metric rewards inaction. Establishing the right metric is not a technical preliminary here; it is the difference between a report that recommends deployment and one that recommends nothing at all.

So whatA do-nothing model scores 99.83% accuracy and catches 0 of 492 frauds. Accuracy is disqualified; average precision, whose random-ranker floor is the fraud rate itself (0.0017), governs every decision in this report.

Fraud occupies a different region of the feature space — 17 of 28 components separate the classes by a large effect

Before any model was fitted, the question was whether fraud is statistically distinguishable at all. It is, decisively. The leading component separates the two classes by 8.1 standard deviations — an effect an order of magnitude beyond the conventional threshold for "large".

So what17 of 28 components clear |d| = 0.8, and V17 reaches 8.1 SD. All 28 confidence intervals exclude zero and the widest spans only 0.185 SD, so the ranking is not sampling noise. 72% of fraud falls below the 1st percentile of legitimate traffic on the top two components simultaneously.
Part II · What kind of fraud is it?

Fraud is a low-value crime, so “fraud caught” has two different answers

The intuition most fraud teams start with is that fraud chases large transactions. The mean supports it; the median contradicts it. With a 25,691-to-22 skew, the median is the statistic that describes the typical case — and it points the other way.

So whatThe median fraud is 9.82 against 22.00 for legitimate traffic (95% bootstrap CI on the difference excludes zero), while the mean says the opposite. Risk is U-shaped: the smallest band runs 3.0x the portfolio rate and the largest 1.6x, with the middle safest. Zero-amount authorisations alone carry 8.3x elevated risk — the classic card-testing signature.

Fraud concentrates in the quiet hours, when legitimate volume is at its minimum

Hour-of-day is one of only two features in this dataset with real-world meaning — and therefore one of the only findings that can be explained to a stakeholder rather than merely acted on.

So whatHours 1-4 carry 5% of transaction volume but 21% of all fraud, peaking at 8.7x the portfolio rate. 6 of 24 hours have a fraud rate whose 95% interval sits entirely above average. Cramer's V is only 0.044, so hour is weak on its own — but the concentration is not chance.
Part III · What should the team do?

Every supervised model clears the single-feature baseline — and the unsupervised cross-check does not

Two baselines were used, and the second is the demanding one: a logistic regression on the single best feature, refit on the training split and scored on the same held-out set as every other model.

So whatXGBoost (default) reaches average precision 0.831 — 54% above the single-feature baseline of 0.541 and 497x the random floor. Class weighting beat in-fold undersampling by 22%, and hyperparameter tuning changed nothing, so the feature signal — not the algorithm — is doing the work.

At 1,000 reviews a day the team catches 86% of fraud cases but only 81% of fraud value

This is the exhibit the decision is made from. A fraud team cannot review 56,000 transactions; it can review a fixed number per day. The question is therefore not "what is the model's precision" but "what does a fixed queue actually catch" — and, because fraud here is low-value, that has two answers.

So whatAt 1,000 reviews/day the queue covers 0.70% of volume, catches 82 of 95 frauds by count and 81% by value, at 20% precision — about 5 reviews per fraud found, and 318 false alarms. The curve is flat beyond 1,000/day, so doubling the team buys little. The 5-point count-value gap means a queue tuned on case count under-protects revenue.

The cost-optimal cutoff is a band, not a number — driven by a cost the business has never measured

A second threshold rule minimises expected cost, charging each missed fraud its own transaction amount. But the cost of a manual review is unknown, so it is swept rather than assumed. A single line here would be false precision.

So whatAcross plausible review costs the optimal alert volume swings from 1,245 to 197 per day and optimal recall from 87% to 76%. The 1,000/day capacity assumption sits inside that band. Measuring the true cost per review would move the recommended threshold by an order of magnitude — far more than any modelling choice in this analysis.

Performance survives the out-of-time test, which is the condition production actually runs under

A random split interleaves test rows with training rows and cannot detect a model that works only on the period it was trained on. Production scoring is always out-of-time, so both splits were run.

So whatTrained on Day 0 and tested on Day 1, the winner scores 0.790 against 0.831 on the random split — a change of -5.0%. Part of that gap is not drift: the temporal model trains on 272 fraud cases rather than 378. The recommendation quotes the temporal figure, because that is the condition the model will operate under.
Part IV · What this analysis cannot do

Three limits bound the deployment — and they are properties of the data, not gaps in the work

The model cannot explain itself All 17 strongly-separating features are unnamed principal components. An analyst cannot be told why a transaction was flagged in any actionable sense, and no fraud-prevention policy can be derived from a PCA loading. This is why the recommendation is a review queue for human adjudication rather than an auto-decline engine.
A fairness audit is impossible, not merely skipped The dataset carries no age, gender, geography, income or tenure field, so protected-subgroup performance cannot be measured at all. The audits that are possible were run: recall varies 20% across amount bands, and overnight recall is 96% against 83% elsewhere. Because the features are anonymized, we also cannot rule out that the model keys on a proxy for a protected attribute — and "we didn't include the attribute" is not a defence.
48 hours cannot evidence stability Concept drift is unmeasurable over two days. The monitoring plan — PSI on the top components, precision-at-capacity tracking, retraining triggers — is specified but unvalidated, which is why 60 days of shadow-mode operation is a precondition of go-live rather than a nice-to-have. Label provenance is also unknown: whether Class means confirmed or merely flagged fraud bounds how far the ground truth can be trusted.
Appendix · Method

Data lineage and preparation

Source: ULB/Kaggle credit-card transactions, 284,807 rows x 31 columns, European cardholders, September 2013, SHA-256 76274b691b16a6c49d3f159c..., verified at every stage. All 14/14 schema rules passed, including the canonical 284,315/492 class split and a PCA mean-zero integrity check. Zero missing values across all 31 columns.

The duplicate decision. 1,081 exact duplicate rows were removed — on leakage grounds, not tidiness. 19 of them were fraud (3.9% of the entire positive class); left in place, identical rows straddling the train/test boundary let the model be re-scored on rows it had memorised. The decision was tested rather than asserted: retaining the duplicates raises average precision to 0.873, an inflation of +0.042 — which is precisely the artificial gain the removal exists to prevent.

Model and validation

XGBoost (default) on 32 features: V1-V28, log-amount, cyclic hour encoding, and an arrival-gap proxy. Raw Time, raw Amount, the day index and a per-class z-score were excluded by design — the last of those because a per-class z-score is target leakage. Class weighting at 599:1 handles the imbalance; in-fold random undersampling was tested as a comparator and lost by 22%. Scaling is fit inside the pipeline on training folds only; the test set was scored once. Permutation importance on the held-out set agrees with the pre-model effect-size ranking on 6 of the top 10 features. The arrival-gap proxy returned a negative importance (rank 31 of 32) and is recommended for removal — a feature that failed its test, reported rather than dropped quietly.

Statistical method

Mann-Whitney U as the primary test (heavy-tailed components), Welch's t as cross-check, Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across 28 comparisons, Cohen's d with 95% analytic CIs, rank-biserial correlation, Cramer's V, and Wilson score intervals for low-count proportions. Effect sizes lead every claim and p-values are not interpreted: at n = 283,726 a component separating the classes by 0.105 SD — a gap no plot could show — still clears p < 0.05. Significance at this sample size measures how much data was collected, not how much a finding matters.

Reproducibility

Six notebooks run in order (01_ingestion → 06_reporting), fixed seed 42 throughout, dependencies pinned in requirements.txt. Every figure in this report is regenerated from stored artifacts and every statistic is read from _key_figures.json, so the report cannot drift out of sync with the analysis. Path C (causal inference) is explicitly out of scope: there is no intervention, no assignment mechanism, and no counterfactual available in this data.
