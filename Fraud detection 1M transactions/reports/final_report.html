Fraud Analytics · Tier A Diagnostic

Fraud here is engineered, not discovered — so we ship a leakage-honest method and a review policy, not a headline score

1,000,000 synthetic transactions · 50,000 accounts · 7 fraud types · 200 rings · seed 42
Executive Summary · SCR

Situation. A synthetic benchmark of 1M card transactions carries a 1.71% fraud base rate, concentrated on familiar risk axes.

Complication. The fraud label is generated from the very features we would score on, so any classifier looks near-perfect — and that number cannot transfer to production.

Resolution. We therefore measure method quality: a temporal, leakage-honest pipeline; an ablation proving the model recovers the generator; and the network graph as the one genuinely independent signal — packaged as a review-capacity policy.

At a 2% review budget, ranking transactions by the model captures 82.1% of all fraud — and the only signal not baked into the generator is the ring/network structure, worth +0.0016 PR-AUC on top of the tabular model.
  1. Fraud is concentrated and predictable. Foreign, high-risk-MCC, unknown-device and overnight transactions each multiply the 1.71% base rate several-fold.
  2. The model recovers the formula, it does not detect fraud. Knocking out the top generator driver (velocity) collapses PR-AUC by 0.221, confirming the model reconstructs the generator rather than discovering fraud.
  3. The network graph is the one independent, transferable signal — ring features add +0.0016 PR-AUC that the transaction formula cannot supply.
Recommendation — Fraud-ops should (a) treat model scores as a ranking for a fixed review budget (start at 2% ≈ 82.1% recall), and (b) invest in entity-resolution / ring features before any GNN, since that is the only lift not attributable to label leakage. Model-risk should re-run the ablation on non-leaky, delayed-confirmation labels before any production claim.

Part I · Diagnostic — where fraud lives

Fraud stacks on a few known axes — foreign and high-risk-MCC transactions lead

So What: a review policy can lead with a handful of flags and cover most exposure — no model strictly required to triage.

The risk is nocturnal — the 00:00–05:00 window runs above the daily mean

So What: auto-hold thresholds and staffing should tighten overnight; a flat 24-hour rule leaves recall on the table at equal cost.

All seven fraud types share one signature — rare types are near-indistinguishable

So What: multi-class typing adds little for low-share patterns; prioritise a strong binary model plus a capacity policy over 7-way classification.

Network rings are a separate signal — ring accounts defraud far above base rate

So What: the graph carries information the transaction formula lacks — the basis for the lift test in Part II.

Appendix · Methodology

How this was produced

Data: synthetic benchmark (5 tables, seed 42). Grain: one row per transaction. Full schema and the leakage register in DOCS/data_dictionary.md.

Split: temporal — train on the earliest 80% by timestamp (≤ 2024-05-26), test on the most recent 20% (≥ 2024-05-26). No random split (avoids temporal leakage).

Model: XGBoost (hist, 250 trees, depth 6, lr 0.1), scale_pos_weight for the 1.71% imbalance — not SMOTE. Baselines: majority-class (PR-AUC = base rate) and class-weighted logistic regression.

Leakage register: account_profiles fraud fields and fraud_pattern are label-derived and excluded from all models (asserted in code).

Limitations: labels are instantaneous and certain (no 30–90 day confirmation lag); rings are static; no chargeback/review-cost data, so no Rupiah ROI. Every performance number is non-transferable by construction. A production analogue owes a subgroup fairness audit and drift monitoring.

Reproducibility: seed 42, pinned requirements.txt, notebooks 01–05 run top-to-bottom.
